Proxy Policy Steering

Announcement thread

🌐 Project page

Can a frozen robot foundation model learn tasks it could never do on its own — without fine-tuning a single weight?

We introduce Proxy Policy Steering (PPS): small proxy policies that steer the frozen base at inference, adding task-specific behavior while keeping its broad priors.

1/8
2

Traditional adaptation method fine-tunes the foundation model’s weights, reshaping its base distribution.

This adapts the behavior, but it can overfit to the limited demonstrations and lose the model’s broad priors.

2/8
3

PPS estimates how task supervision would shift the base’s distribution by training two proxies:

  • Reference proxy πref: distilled from πbase on the task observations.
  • Task proxy πtask: initialized from πref and fine-tuned on the task data.
3/8
4

The residual between πtask and πref captures the task-induced shift. During inference, we steer πbase by adding this residual in velocity space:

vPPS = vbase + γ · (vtask − vref)

injecting task-specific guidance while keeping the base’s broad priors.

4/8
5

Across 12 diverse tasks, PPS delivers large gains over the base — including 0-to-1 jumps on tasks it never solves on its own.

It also beats fine-tuned models, from-scratch specialists, and prior steering baselines.

5/8
6

By keeping the base model untouched, PPS inherits its broad priors and stays robust — it can recover from failures it never saw in the demonstrations.

LoRA fine-tuning, by contrast, overfits to the handful of demos and loses these priors.

6/8
7

Since the proxies are separate networks, PPS can route new modalities like point cloud or audio into the frozen base, enabling new capabilities that are only possible with new sensors.
